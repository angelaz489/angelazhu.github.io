T1Pal

For 10 weeks, I remotely worked alongside peers at The Design Lab at UCSD as part of the T1Pal team. My team and I worked to figure out the best way to help T1Pal present Nightscout in a digestible manner for all users regardless of tech background.

Jun - Aug 2020 (10 weeks)

Background —

Nightscout is a open source, diy project that allows for patients with Type 1 Diabetes to view and share their glucose levels in real-time through multiple platforms (eg. website, smartwatch, widgets). Nighstcout allows for visualization customization and remote monitoring.

T1Pal hosts Nightscout and aims to eliminate the complicated tech portion of the setup of Nightscout and make it simpler and quicker to get to the users Nightscout page.



Problem Statment —

While T1Pal solves many of the technological challenges around data sharing and security, the setup process was driven by technology and complicated by the many options Nightscout and its plugins offer.

So we asked:


How can T1 Pal create a streamlined set up experience that helps support individual diabetes care?

What's The Challenge? —

We quickly realized that designing for a start-up coming from the community is different than designing for an established company. Because there was no established user database and only a couple facebook groups, a big part of our design journey was learning and bringing together all of the scattered pieces of information about T1Pal to create our design challenge for ourselves.

  • Who is using T1Pal?
  • What is and how does T1Pal work?
  • Why is there a problem to solve?
  • How does Nightscout fit into users lives?

Research —

We started off by learning about Nightscout in order to review T1Pal’s current setup and to test it with participants. However, recruitment was difficult because the team had to find ways of reaching out to social media groups and finding 'time' where a potential participant would be receptive to learning more.

While we worked hard to find and scheduled participants, our team also conducted surveys to justify our qualitative data with quantitative data.



Personas —

With our 6 interviewees and responses from our survey, we formed our personas.





For the purposes of T1Pal, our target audience focuses on the first two users, Jennifer and Annie. We chose not to focus on Steve because these group of people would most likely figure out how to troubleshoot things on their own, and even provide help to the community.

Ideation Goals —

Based on the user testing we did on the current T1Pal, we had some goals in mind when coming up with our prototypes.

  • Reduce confusion of plugin presets
  • Give the user a sense of completion
  • Reduce complicated terminology

New User Flow —

To tackle the plugin presets choices not being intuitive, we looked back at the user flow.

As you can see the user flow of the current T1Pal has many points of decision that the user has to make throughout the process and is very plugin preset oriented.



The new t1pal, which is goal oriented, makes it so that the user will only need to select the devices they already use and just input information when needed. This eliminates any ambiguity during the setup process.

Iterations —

Users input their devices rather than having them choose what presets of plugins they would like to use. Inputting their devices allows for the backend to determine what presents the user would need, rather than having the user figure it out.

Edited the layout of the ‘uploaders’ page and added more instructions so that user would have step by step instructions on how to utilize the links and qr code.

Changed the mascot and theme of t1pal to be more welcoming and friendly for new users. Wanted to illustrate to the user how their data is going into the cloud so less tech savvy users could better understand and visualize what happens to their data as they go through the setup process.

Something an interviewee mentioned during user testing embodied what we were trying to get at with this new owl theme. They liked how it was animated and inviting. They appreciated that it was “more jovial instead of depressing” and “brought positivity than negativity” into the process.
